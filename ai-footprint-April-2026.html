AI · Energy · Water · Explained

How much does AI really cost the planet?

When you ask ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini a question, somewhere a computer is running — drawing electricity and often using water to keep cool. Here's what those invisible costs add up to, in everyday terms, plus what the companies are promising to do about it.

◆ Data current as of spring 2026 ◆ Written for general readers

A few words you'll see a lot

AI model
The "brain" of a chatbot — a piece of software like ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini that can write, answer questions, and generate images.
Training
Building the AI. A one-time, massive computer job that "teaches" the model by feeding it huge amounts of text. Uses enormous power, over weeks or months.
Inference (or "a query")
Using the AI. Every time you type a question and get an answer, that's one query. Each uses a tiny bit of power — but billions happen every day.
Data center
A warehouse-sized building packed with thousands of powerful computers. All those computers run hot and need cooling — which is where the water comes in.
Watt-hour (Wh)
A measure of electricity. A microwave running for about 1 second uses roughly 0.3 Wh. A typical home uses around 29,000 Wh every day.
Megawatt-hour (MWh)
One million watt-hours. It's how we measure big power uses. One average US home uses about 10.6 MWh in a whole year.
Gigawatt (GW)
One billion watts of power. A large nuclear power plant produces about 1 GW. New AI data centers being planned will need 2–5 GW each.
Carbon negative / Net zero
Promises to cancel out (or remove more than) all greenhouse gases a company emits. Microsoft and Google have pledged to hit this by 2030.
Water positive
Putting more clean water back into communities than the company's operations consume.
Closed-loop cooling
A newer system where the same water circulates and cools the computers over and over, instead of evaporating away. Uses far less water.
01

What one question to an AI actually uses

The four biggest AI companies are Google, OpenAI (maker of ChatGPT), Anthropic (maker of Claude), and Microsoft (which sells Copilot but runs OpenAI's models underneath). Here's what it takes to answer one typical question on each.

Quick orientation — Electricity 0.3 watt-hours is the kind of tiny number that's hard to picture. Think of it this way: watching nine seconds of television uses about the same electricity as one Google AI question. A single LED light bulb, left on for 3 minutes, uses roughly the same as a whole ChatGPT answer.
Quick orientation — Water A milliliter is about 20 drops from a medicine dropper. The most efficient AI query in this guide (Google Gemini at 0.26 mL) uses roughly five drops — less than what clings to your hands after a quick rinse. The heaviest (Claude Opus at 7.7 mL) is about 1.5 teaspoons. For context, a single toilet flush uses around 6,000 mL — equivalent to about 800 Claude Opus queries.
Quick orientation — Carbon A gram of CO₂ is genuinely tiny. The lightest query here (Google at 0.03 g) is less than what your lungs exhale in one breath. Even the heaviest (Claude Opus at 1.8 g) is roughly what a typical car emits driving about 15 meters. For scale: a round-trip flight from New York to Los Angeles produces around 1,000,000 g (one metric tonne) of CO₂ per passenger.

Compared to just Googling it

The most intuitive benchmark isn't a household — it's a plain web search. Google has published that a traditional Google search uses about 0.3 watt-hours. Here's how each AI query stacks up against that.

The surprising result A simple question to Google Gemini uses less electricity than a traditional Google search — 0.24 Wh versus 0.3 Wh. That's partly because Google has gotten extremely efficient at AI inference, and partly because a web search still has to crawl, rank, and serve an entire results page. But the moment you switch to a larger model like Claude Opus, you're at 13× a regular search.

02

The one-time cost: building the AI

Before an AI can answer any question, it has to be "trained" — a massive computer job that takes weeks or months and gulps enormous amounts of electricity. This happens once per model.

The key shift Training used to be the biggest energy cost. That's no longer true. With billions of people now asking questions every day, the day-to-day use (inference) has overtaken training as the main energy drain. Building the AI is a one-time shock; running it is the ongoing bill.

04

What each company is promising

The four companies are at very different stages of talking about — and doing something about — their environmental footprint. Microsoft and Google publish detailed annual reports. OpenAI has only a blog post. Anthropic hasn't published a sustainability report at all.

Microsoft behind schedule

Promised in 2020: carbon negative, water positive, and zero waste by 2030. Also: remove from the atmosphere all the carbon the company has ever emitted since 1975, by 2050.

  • 2025Hit goal: all their electricity matched with clean-energy contracts
  • NowBuilding a new data-center design that uses essentially zero water — circulates the same water over and over. Saves ~33 million gallons per facility per year.
  • NowHas contracts for 34 gigawatts of renewable energy across 24 countries
  • ⚠ ProblemTotal emissions have actually gone up 23–30% since 2020 because of AI buildout — the opposite direction of the promise
Named initiatives that could help
  • Three Mile Island restart835 MW · Pennsylvania · online 2028 — Constellation Energy restarting the closed nuclear plant under a 20-year power purchase agreement with Microsoft. The $1.6B project received a $1B DOE loan in late 2025.
  • Helion fusion plant50 MW · Chelan County, WA · target 2028 — The world's first commercial fusion power agreement. Construction began July 2025. If it delivers, entirely carbon-free baseload power.
  • Brookfield Renewable framework10.5 GW · US & Europe · 2026–2030 — The largest corporate renewable energy deal ever signed, covering new wind and solar capacity.
  • Gaia carbon capture~3 million tons CO₂ · Copenhagen — Industrial capture at a waste-to-energy plant, targeting the historical-emissions removal promise.
Reality check: 2024 was the first year emissions dipped slightly — still 23% above the 2020 starting point. Removing every ton of CO₂ emitted since 1975 by 2050 requires carbon removal technology at roughly 50–100× today's global scale.
Technologies that could change the math
Submer immersion coolingavailable now — Submerging servers in dielectric fluid cuts cooling energy ~50% and uses 99% less water than traditional air cooling. Intel-certified and deployable today; addresses Microsoft's water-positive goal directly.
Climeworks Cypress DAC Hubconstruction 2026 — Industrial-scale direct air capture plant in Louisiana (DOE-funded, 3rd-gen Climeworks technology). Microsoft already holds purchase agreements. When operational, provides verifiable CO₂ removal credits at megaton scale — the only path to "carbon negative."
Form Energy iron-air batteriespilot 2025–26 — 100-hour discharge storage at a target cost of ~$20/kWh (vs. $100+ for lithium-ion). Firms up wind and solar to run 24/7 without natural gas backup — essential if nuclear restarts are delayed.

Google behind schedule

Promised: net-zero emissions and water positive by 2030.

  • 2023Hit goal: electricity matched with clean-energy contracts
  • NowCut the electricity per AI question by 33× in just 12 months — a genuinely huge efficiency win
  • NowMost efficient data centers in the industry; water use per unit of energy down 40% since 2021
  • 2030sBuilding partnerships for small nuclear reactors (with a company called Kairos Power and the Tennessee Valley Authority) — new clean power specifically for AI
  • ⚠ ProblemTotal emissions are up 51% since 2019, also driven by AI growth
Named initiatives that could help
  • Kairos Power SMRs with TVA500 MW total · Oak Ridge, TN · first reactor 2030 — The first utility in the US to sign a binding deal with a next-generation small modular reactor company. Power goes directly to Google data centers in Tennessee and Alabama.
  • NextEra Duane Arnold nuclear restart615 MW · Cedar Rapids, IA · online 2029 — 25-year power purchase agreement; Google plans up to 6 data centers near the facility.
  • Elementl Power advanced nuclear1.8 GW · 3 US sites · target late 2020s — Google provides early-stage capital; Elementl selects the reactor technology per site. Elementl's broader goal is 10+ GW by 2035.
  • Intersect Power acquisition$4.75B · announced Dec 2025 — Brings clean energy development fully in-house rather than relying on third-party PPAs.
Reality check: Google quietly moved its net-zero 2030 goal from its main sustainability page to a report appendix in 2024 — a sign of internal doubt. Data center efficiency dropped emissions 12% in 2024, but electricity consumption grew 27% the same year. The efficiency gains are real; the volume growth is faster.
Technologies that could change the math
Fervo Energy enhanced geothermalcontracted · online late 2026 — Google signed a 115 MW PPA for Fervo's Cape Station in Utah; Phase 2 targets 400 MW by 2028. Unlike solar or wind, geothermal runs 24/7 with no storage needed and uses minimal water — solving both the carbon-free and water-positive gaps at once.
DeepMind AlphaControlalready deployed — Machine learning system that optimizes data center cooling in real time. Already cuts cooling energy ~40% across Google's own facilities. Represents one of the few efficiency wins that actually shows up in the numbers today.
Form Energy iron-air batteriespilot 2025–26 — 100-hour discharge at ~$20/kWh target cost. Enables Google's solar and wind contracts to cover overnight and multi-day gaps — the missing piece for hitting 24/7 carbon-free energy on every grid, every hour.

OpenAI no formal plan

No published climate goal. No target date for carbon neutrality, no water commitment, no annual sustainability report.

  • NowCEO Sam Altman publicly says AI needs to move to nuclear, wind, and solar — but this isn't a binding promise
  • NowSays most of their data centers now use closed-loop cooling (water is recycled, not evaporated)
  • 2025+"Stargate" project — a roughly $500 billion data-center buildout with Oracle, SoftBank, and others
  • 2026+New 800-acre campus in Abilene, Texas; uses 8 million gallons of water to initially fill the cooling system
  • ⚠ ProblemWithout published numbers, nobody can verify efficiency claims
Named initiatives that could help
  • SB Energy solar + battery partnershipmulti-GW · $1B investment · 2026–2027 — SoftBank subsidiary building dedicated solar and battery storage campuses for Stargate data centers. First site in Milam County, Texas (1.2 GW) targets 2026.
  • Stargate five-site buildout~7 GW across TX, and 3 additional sites — Each site paired with renewable generation, though natural gas remains a component for baseload reliability.
  • Small modular reactors (planned)no named partner yet — Sam Altman has publicly stated SMRs are essential for Stargate baseload, but unlike Google and Microsoft, OpenAI has not signed a deal with a specific nuclear company as of early 2026.
Reality check: OpenAI has no carbon target, no water commitment, and no sustainability report. The Stargate buildout is primarily a compute strategy, not an environmental one. Without named nuclear partners or binding emissions goals, the clean-energy framing remains aspirational.
Technologies that could change the math
Oklo Aurora micro-reactorsNRC license review — Sam Altman personally backed Oklo; their Aurora units produce ~15 MW each and can be sited directly next to data centers without new long-distance transmission. A cluster of units could provide firm baseload for a Stargate site. NRC licensing is the main bottleneck.
X-energy Xe-100 TRISO reactorsAmazon deploying 2027–28 — Amazon is already investing in these 76 MW units for AWS data centers. TRISO fuel is physically incapable of melting (tolerates temperatures above 1,600°C), sharply reducing regulatory risk. OpenAI could follow Amazon's lead and contract capacity from the same vendor.
NVIDIA TensorRT-LLM speculative decodingavailable now — A technique where a small "draft" model predicts multiple tokens at once, which the main model verifies in a single pass. Delivers 2–3.6× inference throughput with no accuracy loss — meaning the same Stargate hardware answers 2–3× as many questions for the same electricity.

Anthropic no formal plan

No published sustainability report. Runs Claude on Amazon's and Google's data centers, so Anthropic's footprint is effectively whatever those clouds are.

  • NowIndependent studies rank Claude as the most efficient of the big AI models per unit of performance
  • 2027Projects it will need a dedicated 2 GW data center for one training run
  • 2028Projects a 5 GW training data center, and advocates for 50 GW of US AI capacity total
  • ⚠ PositionTreats building out this much energy infrastructure as a national priority versus China, rather than primarily a climate question
Named initiatives that could help
  • Amazon Indiana campus1,200 acres · $11B · already operational — Dedicated infrastructure built and operated by Amazon Web Services for Anthropic's workloads. Anthropic's footprint here is effectively whatever AWS's energy mix is.
  • Fluidstack data centersNew York & Texas · $50B commitment · 2026 — Custom-designed facilities coming online throughout 2026, built specifically for Anthropic's training and inference workloads.
  • Grid cost offset commitmentno cap disclosed — Anthropic has pledged to cover 100% of grid upgrade costs for interconnecting its data centers, and to offset electricity price increases for consumers in regions where its demand outpaces local generation — a notably honest acknowledgment of grid strain.
Reality check: Anthropic's approach is more candid than its peers — it acknowledges grid strain and commits to covering consumer costs — but it is reactive rather than proactive. There are no named renewable or nuclear deals, no carbon targets, and no pathway to net-zero. The company's own projections (5 GW for a single training run by 2028) imply AI training at frontier scale is fundamentally incompatible with near-term climate goals.
Technologies that could change the math
AWS Trainium2 chipsin production — Amazon's custom AI accelerator, purpose-built for training large models. Roughly 2× more energy-efficient than equivalent GPU alternatives. Because Anthropic runs on AWS, every shift toward Trainium2 directly reduces Claude's training footprint without any infrastructure changes on Anthropic's end.
Speculative decoding (vLLM / TensorRT-LLM)available now — A widely-adopted inference technique where a small cheap model drafts tokens and the main model batch-verifies them. Cuts active compute per output token by 2–3× at no quality cost. Already in production at several major labs; Anthropic deploying this would immediately reduce inference energy per Claude query.
Fervo Energy geothermal via AWSAWS contracted · online 2026 — AWS has its own geothermal power purchase agreements with Fervo Energy. Since Anthropic's workloads run on AWS infrastructure, those clean-energy contracts reduce Anthropic's effective carbon footprint even without a direct deal — one of the few areas where running on a cloud provider is an environmental advantage.

05

What it all actually means

Five honest takeaways

1. Individual use is basically harmless.

If you personally use ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini every day — even heavily — your AI use is a tiny fraction of your total environmental footprint. One shower probably uses more water than a year's worth of questions. Driving to the store uses more gas than a year of chatting with an AI. This is not the place to feel guilty.

2. The efficiency gains are real — and enormous.

3. The total footprint is exploding anyway.

Because usage is growing faster than efficiency. Google and Microsoft both pledged to be carbon-negative or net-zero by 2030; both are instead seeing their total emissions go up by 30–51% since their starting points. Without a major shift, those 2030 promises will not be met.

4. The backup plan depends on things that don't fully exist yet.

Microsoft's zero-water cooling design is new and not yet at scale. Google's nuclear partnerships are for reactors that haven't been built yet. Much of what's being promised for 2030 relies on technology that has to arrive on a very tight schedule. Historically, it usually doesn't.

5. The real problem is coming, fast.

Anthropic itself projects the US will need 50 gigawatts of new power capacity for AI by 2028 — roughly four times the peak power demand of New York City. Whether we build that with nuclear, natural gas, or renewables is the decision that will actually shape the environmental story of AI. That decision is being made now, mostly outside public view.
