Concept Needs vs. wants (the honest version)

A need is what keeps you alive, housed, and employed. A want is everything else — and that's fine. The trick isn't "stop wanting things"; it's knowing which is which so you can choose on purpose.

Needs: rent/housing, groceries, utilities, basic transportation to work/school, required medicine, essential clothing.
Wants: restaurants, streaming, nice-to-have tech, brand upgrades, entertainment, convenience.

Wants aren't the enemy. A life with zero wants is grim. The goal is knowing the ratio so you can enjoy the wants without anxiety.

Concept Paycheck-to-paycheck vs. saver lifestyle

Living paycheck-to-paycheck means each month's money arrives, gets spent, and then you wait. Saver lifestyle means some of each paycheck is automatically routed to savings before you see it — so spending what's left is guilt-free.

It's like eating. If you put dessert on the table first, you'll fill up on it and skip the vegetables. Pay yourself first — route savings before spending sees the money — and the "sacrifice" disappears because you never had that money in your checking account to begin with.

The move isn't "spend less." It's "redirect the flow so the default is saving." You'll feel the shift in 1–3 months, not years.

Myth "I'll save when I make more money."

Research and anecdote both show: people who don't save on $2,500/mo usually don't save on $5,000/mo either — they just spend more. The habit is the skill. Starting with $20/paycheck builds the same muscle as $2,000/paycheck.

Smart Questions Before any purchase over $100

  1. Is this a need or a want? (Either is okay — name it honestly.)
  2. If I waited 48 hours, would I still want it?
  3. Could I buy it used, borrow, or rent first?
  4. Does it replace a cost I'm already paying, or add a new one?
  5. What's the total cost — including subscriptions, maintenance, or insurance?

Concept Gross vs. net pay

Gross pay is the pizza; net pay is the slices left after taxes, insurance, and retirement each take their piece. The first time you see a paycheck, the difference is shocking — that's normal.

  • Gross: total earned before anything is taken out.
  • Net (take-home): what actually hits your bank account.
  • Budget off net, not gross. Always.

Concept Pre-tax vs. post-tax deductions

Pre-tax comes out before taxes are calculated. That means you're taxed on a smaller number — so a pre-tax dollar into retirement or health insurance "costs" you less than a dollar of take-home.

Pre-tax is like getting a discount at the register before sales tax is added. Post-tax is paying full price, then the discount.

Common pre-tax items: 401(k) contributions, traditional IRA (sometimes), health insurance premiums, HSA/FSA, commuter benefits.
Common post-tax items: Roth 401(k), life insurance beyond basic, union dues (varies), disability insurance.

Concept The W-4 form

The W-4 is the form you fill out at a new job that tells your employer how much federal tax to withhold from each check. Get it wrong, and at tax time you either owe a big bill or loaned the government your money interest-free.

  • Single filer, one job, no dependents: the default is usually close enough.
  • Multiple jobs or a working spouse: use the IRS estimator (irs.gov/individuals/tax-withholding-estimator) — otherwise you'll under-withhold.
  • Update it when life changes: marriage, kid, second job, big raise.

Myth "A big tax refund means I did taxes well."

A refund means you overpaid all year — you gave the government an interest-free loan. Breaking even is ideal. Small refund is fine. Huge refund means your W-4 is off — fix it and get that money in every paycheck instead.

Smart Questions At your first (or any new) job

  1. What's the pay period? Weekly, bi-weekly, semi-monthly, monthly?
  2. When is my first paycheck — and is it a full one?
  3. Is there a 401(k) match? If yes, what's the formula and vesting schedule?
  4. What's the health insurance premium and when does coverage start?
  5. How do I submit a W-4 — and when can I update it?
  6. Is overtime paid? (non-exempt hourly vs. salaried exempt)

Takeaway

Your gross pay is a headline number. Your net pay is your life. Understand the deductions in between — especially the pre-tax ones — and you'll pay less tax while saving more.

Concept Why taxes exist

Taxes are the cover charge for public infrastructure. Roads, public schools, fire departments, courts, the military, national parks, Social Security, Medicare — none of it is free. Everyone who earns chips in, and the rate scales with income.

You'll pay several layers: federal income tax, state income tax (in most states), FICA (Social Security + Medicare), and local taxes (sales tax, property tax if you own a home).

Myth "Getting a raise can push me into a higher bracket and I'll take home less."

Completely false — and so common it costs people raises and promotions. Brackets are marginal: only the dollars above each threshold are taxed at the higher rate. A raise always increases your take-home. Use the widget below to see it in action.

Concept Marginal vs. effective tax rate

  • Marginal rate: the rate on your last dollar earned. If you're in the 22% bracket, only dollars above the 12% threshold are taxed at 22%.
  • Effective rate: total tax ÷ total income. Always lower than marginal.

Think of brackets like buckets. Each bucket holds a fixed amount of income at a specific rate. When one overflows, the extra spills into the next higher bucket — but the dollars already in the lower buckets stay there.

Concept W-2 vs. 1099

W-2 (employee)

  • Employer withholds taxes each paycheck
  • Employer pays half of Social Security/Medicare
  • Eligible for benefits: health, 401(k), PTO
  • File Form 1040 at tax time

1099 (contractor)

  • No withholding — you must set aside ~25–30%
  • You pay both halves of FICA (self-employment tax = 15.3%)
  • No employer benefits
  • File Schedule C + quarterly estimated taxes

Rule of thumb: if you're 1099, mentally slice ~30% off every check and park it in a separate savings account so tax day doesn't wreck you.

Concept Standard deduction vs. itemizing

Everyone gets a standard deduction — a chunk of income the IRS doesn't tax (roughly $14,600 single in 2024; check current). Only itemize if your deductible expenses (mortgage interest, big charitable giving, state taxes) add up to more than that. For most young adults: the standard deduction wins.

Concept Credits you should know about

  • Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC) — for low-to-moderate income workers. Often missed.
  • American Opportunity Credit / Lifetime Learning Credit — college tuition and fees.
  • Saver's Credit — bonus for low-income earners who contribute to a 401(k) or IRA.

Credits > deductions. A $1,000 credit cuts your tax by $1,000. A $1,000 deduction cuts your taxable income by $1,000, saving only your marginal rate (e.g., $120 at the 12% bracket).

Smart Questions Before you file taxes

  1. Do I have all my W-2s, 1099s, 1098-T (tuition), and 1098-E (student loan interest)?
  2. Am I eligible for any credits (EITC, education, Saver's)?
  3. Was anyone else allowed to claim me as a dependent? (Affects which credits you can take.)
  4. Should I use free filing (IRS Free File, Free Fillable Forms, VITA if income-eligible)?
  5. Did I contribute to an HSA / IRA last year that I should report?
  6. If I moved, do I owe tax in more than one state?

Takeaway

Your marginal rate is not your effective rate. Raises always help. Credits beat deductions. And pre-tax contributions (401(k), HSA) are the cleanest way to cut your tax bill on purpose.

Concept What a budget actually is

A budget is a plan for your money — not a cage. It's the difference between driving with a map and driving with your eyes closed. You'll still get lost sometimes; the map helps you notice and course-correct.

The best budget is the one you'll actually keep using in three months. Elaborate systems fail. Simple systems with automation win.

Concept Three budgeting styles (pick one)

50 / 30 / 20

50% of net pay to needs (rent, groceries, utilities, required transport). 30% to wants. 20% to savings + debt payoff. Good starting framework — no tracking required beyond the three buckets.

Zero-based

Every dollar of net pay gets assigned a job before the month starts. Income − expenses − savings = $0. More work, more control. Apps like YNAB live here.

Pay-yourself-first

Decide your savings percentage. Automate it off the top. Spend the rest however you want without guilt. The lightest possible system.

Concept Automation beats willpower

Willpower is a muscle that gets tired. Automation is a machine that never does. Once you set it up, you don't need to feel motivated — the system is motivated for you.

The two-transfer habit that changes everything:

  1. Direct deposit split. Ask HR to send part of each paycheck directly to savings. You never see it in checking — can't spend what isn't there.
  2. Auto-transfer day after payday. If #1 isn't possible, schedule a recurring transfer to savings for the day after payday.

Smart Questions When your budget feels broken

  1. Is one category (usually rent or a car) over 35% of my take-home? That's the structural problem — no tweak will fix it.
  2. Am I counting any annual or irregular expenses (car registration, holiday gifts, travel)? Divide by 12 and save monthly.
  3. Is there a subscription I'm not using? Cancel. Check statements every 3 months.
  4. Am I using the budget as a tool, or a guilt-stick? The budget exists for your life; your life doesn't exist for the budget.

Takeaway

The goal isn't to feel deprived — it's to route money before your brain touches it. Automate savings first, spend the rest without guilt, and tune the percentages once a year.

Concept Why you need an emergency fund

An emergency fund is a financial airbag. You hope you never use it, but the one time you do, it saves you from slamming into a debt cycle. A $600 car repair with no fund becomes a $900 credit card balance with 24% APR. A $600 car repair with a fund is just a bad Tuesday.

Without a buffer, every surprise becomes a crisis, and every crisis becomes debt. The emergency fund is the single highest-leverage thing you can build early.

Concept How much? Build it in steps.

  1. Starter fund: $500–$1,000. Covers most small emergencies. Build this first, before extra debt payoff.
  2. One-month fund: enough to cover all needs for a single month. Breathing room.
  3. Full fund: 3–6 months of expenses. Covers a job loss, medical issue, or major repair. Aim for 3 if you have stable income and low fixed costs; 6 if your income is variable, you have dependents, or you're self-employed.

Don't try to do all three at once. Stack them.

Concept Where to keep it (HYSA)

High-Yield Savings Account (HYSA) — online-only savings accounts that pay 10–20× the interest of big-bank savings, still FDIC-insured, and accessible in 1–2 business days.

If a regular bank savings is a locker at school, a HYSA is the same locker but it pays you to store things. Same function, way better rate.

  • Keep it separate from your daily checking so you don't "accidentally" spend it.
  • Not in the stock market. The whole point is no risk of loss when you need it.
  • Accepted providers (examples, not endorsements): any major online bank offering FDIC insurance and competitive APY. Compare at consumerfinance.gov.

Concept Sinking funds (mini-savings for known expenses)

A sinking fund is savings for a known future expense — not emergencies. Car registration, holiday gifts, a summer trip, annual car insurance. Divide the total by months until due, save that much monthly. When it's time, you pay from the fund — no scramble, no credit card.

Emergency fund = airbag. Sinking funds = putting aside gas money before the road trip, not at the pump with a prayer.

Smart Questions Before opening a savings account

  1. What's the APY (annual percentage yield)?
  2. Is it FDIC-insured up to $250,000?
  3. Are there monthly fees or minimum balance requirements?
  4. How many free transfers per month? (Used to be legally capped at 6; some banks still limit.)
  5. How long do transfers to my checking take — same day, 1 day, 3 days?

Concept Banks vs. credit unions

Credit Union

  • Member-owned, nonprofit
  • Usually lower fees, better rates
  • Smaller network — check ATM access
  • NCUA-insured (equivalent to FDIC)

Big Bank

  • For-profit, shareholders
  • Huge ATM/branch network
  • More fees; lower savings rates
  • FDIC-insured

Common setup: credit union (or a no-fee online bank) for daily checking + an online HYSA for savings. Best of both worlds.

Red Flag Fees Things to watch for

  • Monthly maintenance fee ($5–$15) — avoidable at most no-fee online banks and credit unions. No one should pay this.
  • Overdraft fee ($25–$35 per transaction) — opt out of overdraft "protection" so the transaction is declined instead. You'll lose a purchase attempt, not $35.
  • ATM fees — use your bank's network or get reimbursed. Some online banks refund ATM fees.
  • Minimum balance fee — penalty for not keeping enough money in the account. Absurd.
  • Paper statement fee — switch to electronic.
  • Wire transfer fee ($15–$30) — use ACH or Zelle instead when possible.

Concept FDIC / NCUA insurance

The government insures up to $250,000 per depositor, per bank, per ownership category. If the bank fails, your money is protected up to that limit. Always verify your bank or credit union is FDIC / NCUA insured — there's a badge on their site.

Concept Debit vs. credit card (for payments)

  • Debit = pulls from your checking immediately. Fraud protection is weaker — lost money can take weeks to recover.
  • Credit = bank pays the merchant; you pay the bank later. Strong fraud protection (you're not out money while disputing). Builds credit history.

If you can pay a credit card off every month, use it for most purchases instead of debit. Better fraud protection, better rewards, builds credit. If you can't, stick with debit until you build that discipline — carrying a balance at 20%+ APR erases every reward you'd earn.

Smart Questions Before opening any account

  1. Is there a monthly fee? How do I avoid it?
  2. What's the minimum opening deposit?
  3. Is there a minimum balance to avoid fees?
  4. What's the overdraft policy? Can I opt out?
  5. How do I move money in and out — and what's the speed?
  6. Is it FDIC or NCUA insured?
  7. What's the APY, and can it change?

Takeaway

Keep checking at a no-fee institution. Keep emergency savings at an HYSA. Opt out of overdraft protection. Never pay a monthly fee. If your bank charges one, switch — it's a 20-minute task that pays you forever.

Concept What a credit score actually is

A credit score is your financial GPA. It's a 3-digit number (300–850) summarizing how reliably you've paid back borrowed money. Lenders use it to decide whether to lend, how much, and at what interest rate. A higher score = cheaper loans = real money.

The main score brand is FICO; VantageScore is the other. Both use similar factors.

  • 800+ — Excellent. Best rates on everything.
  • 740–799 — Very good. You'll qualify for almost anything.
  • 670–739 — Good. Most approvals, average rates.
  • 580–669 — Fair. Higher rates, some rejections.
  • Below 580 — Poor. Expensive loans, many denials.

Concept Credit utilization, explained

Utilization is how "full" your credit card looks. If your limit is $1,000 and you carry a $900 balance, you look 90% maxed-out — to a lender, that's a person stretched thin. Same $900 on a $5,000 limit is 18% — a person in control.

Your statement date (not due date) is what reports to bureaus. Two pro moves:

  1. Pay before the statement closes to report a lower balance.
  2. Ask for a credit limit increase every 6–12 months (no hard pull if the issuer allows soft). Higher limit, same spending = lower utilization.

Concept Your first credit card — the smart way

  1. Start with a secured card if you have no credit history. You deposit (say $200) and that becomes your limit. Pay on time — graduate to unsecured in 6–12 months.
  2. Student cards are also a clean entry point.
  3. Use it for one recurring small expense (e.g., a $15 streaming subscription). Autopay the full balance each month. Credit builds quietly in the background.
  4. Never carry a balance month-to-month if you can help it. Interest on cards (20%+ APR) eats any reward you could earn.
  5. Check your free credit reports annually at annualcreditreport.com — the only federally authorized free source.

Myth "Carrying a small balance helps my score."

False. You build credit by using the card and paying it off, not by leaving a balance. Paying in full avoids interest and still reports activity to the bureaus. Carrying a balance just donates interest to the bank.

Smart Questions Before signing up for any credit card

  1. Is there an annual fee? Is the reward worth it for my spending?
  2. What's the APR? (Doesn't matter if you always pay in full, but does if things go sideways.)
  3. What's the rewards structure — flat-rate or category bonuses?
  4. Is there a sign-up bonus, and can I hit the minimum-spend without overspending?
  5. Are there foreign transaction fees? (Matters if you travel.)
  6. Does it report to all 3 bureaus (Experian, Equifax, TransUnion)?

Takeaway

Your credit score is a slow-moving number built by boring consistency: pay on time, keep utilization low, don't close your oldest account. Do those three things for 7+ years and you'll have an excellent score — which saves you tens of thousands on future mortgages and auto loans.

Concept Not all debt is the same

Debt is a tool. A hammer builds a house or breaks a window — depends how you use it. The question isn't "is debt bad?" but "is this debt buying me an asset that appreciates or a skill that earns, or is it renting me a lifestyle I can't afford?"

Potentially good debt

  • Mortgage (buys an asset; rates are low historically)
  • Federal student loans (at reasonable totals, for an earning degree)
  • Small-business loan with a real plan

Usually bad debt

  • Credit card balances carried month-to-month (20%+ APR)
  • Payday loans (~400% APR — a trap)
  • Buy-now-pay-later used for impulse wants
  • Car loans over 60 months on depreciating vehicles
  • Private student loans beyond necessity

Concept APR vs. APY (know the difference)

  • APR (Annual Percentage Rate) — the rate you pay on borrowed money. Lower is better. Always quoted for loans and cards.
  • APY (Annual Percentage Yield) — the rate you earn on savings/investments. Higher is better. Includes compounding.

APR is the price of renting money; APY is the wage money earns for you. Never confuse them — lenders sometimes advertise low APRs next to their "APY" savings rates; look at which is which.

Concept Two payoff strategies: Avalanche vs. Snowball

Avalanche (math-optimal)

Minimum on all debts. Extra to the highest APR first. When it's gone, roll to the next highest.

Pros: saves the most in interest, fastest mathematically.
Cons: if the highest-rate debt is also your biggest, early wins feel invisible.

Snowball (psychology-optimal)

Minimum on all debts. Extra to the smallest balance first. Knock them out one at a time.

Pros: quick wins build momentum; proven to help people actually finish.
Cons: pays slightly more interest than avalanche.

The right answer is whichever one you'll stick with. Avalanche is cheaper; snowball is more motivating. Both beat no plan.

Concept Minimum payments are a trap

Credit card minimums are calibrated to keep you in debt for decades. A $3,000 balance at 20% APR, paying only minimums (~2% of balance), takes roughly 13 years to pay off and costs more than $3,000 in interest alone. Always pay more than the minimum.

Concept Student loans (quick orientation)

  • Federal loans (Direct Subsidized/Unsubsidized, PLUS) — fixed rates, income-driven repayment plans, potential forgiveness programs. Exhaust these before private loans.
  • Private loans — bank-issued, variable or fixed, fewer protections. Use only as a last resort.
  • Interest on federal unsubsidized loans accrues while you're in school; subsidized loans don't (until grace period ends).
  • Before refinancing federal → private to get a lower rate, understand you lose federal protections (IDR, forbearance, forgiveness).

Deeper student-loan strategy (IDR plans, PSLF, refinancing tradeoffs) is coming in Phase 2.

Smart Questions Before taking on any debt

  1. What's the APR, and is it fixed or variable?
  2. What's the total repayment if I pay as scheduled? (Not just monthly — total paid.)
  3. Is there a prepayment penalty?
  4. Is this debt tied to an asset that appreciates or a skill that earns — or am I financing a lifestyle?
  5. What's my exit plan if income drops?
  6. For cards specifically: what's the deferred-interest catch on "0% for 12 months" offers? (Miss one payment and all accumulated interest is due.)

Takeaway

Any debt above ~8% APR is a financial fire — put it out before investing beyond an employer match. Pick avalanche or snowball, automate the extra payment, and don't look at the total balance daily — look at the trend monthly.

Concept Investing vs. saving (not the same thing)

Saving is keeping money in a safe — it stays the same, just slightly warmer from a little interest. Investing is planting money in a garden — some years it grows, some years it shrinks, but over long time horizons the garden has always gotten bigger.

  • Save for anything you'll need in < 2 years (emergency fund, down payment, next semester's tuition). HYSA.
  • Invest for anything 5+ years out (retirement, long-term goals). Stock market.
  • The middle (2–5 years) — bonds, CDs, or short-term investments. Mixed.

Concept Compounding is the whole game

You saw it in the Compound Time Machine. When returns earn returns, the curve goes vertical in later decades. The #1 way to lose is to start late. The #1 way to win is to start early and keep going.

Rule of 72: divide 72 by your annual return to estimate years to double. At 7%, money doubles every ~10 years. Start at 22 with $5,000 invested and never add another dime → roughly $80,000 by 62 (at 7%).

Concept The free-money rule: employer 401(k) match

If your job offers a 401(k) match (e.g., "we match 50% up to the first 6% you contribute"), contribute at least enough to get the full match. Anything less is leaving free salary on the table. This is usually the highest-return "investment" you'll ever make — an instant 50–100% return.

Concept Index funds, in one paragraph

An index fund owns tiny pieces of hundreds or thousands of companies automatically. Instead of picking a winning stock, you own "the market." Historically ~7% average annual return after inflation, extremely low fees, and decades of evidence that they beat most professional stock-pickers. For most people, most of the time, boring broad-market index funds are the right answer.

Myth "I need to wait until I have more money to invest."

Many brokerages let you buy fractional shares with $1. $25/month into a broad index fund, starting at 22, grows to meaningfully more by 65 than $200/month starting at 40. You don't need "more money" — you need more time in the market.

Smart Questions Before opening an investment account

  1. Do I have an emergency fund first? (If not, pause and build that.)
  2. Is there an employer 401(k) match I'm not capturing? (Get the free money before anything else.)
  3. What are the fees / expense ratios? Under 0.10% is great; over 0.50% is expensive.
  4. What's the minimum investment?
  5. Am I comfortable leaving this money alone for at least 5 years?
  6. What's my investor type? (Phase 2 helps figure this out.)

Takeaway

You don't need a finance degree to invest successfully. The winning formula for most people is embarrassingly simple: start early, capture the match, use tax-advantaged accounts, buy broad index funds, don't sell in a panic. Phase 2 of this module will teach you each piece in depth.

Concept The sticker price is the smallest part

A car is an iceberg: the sticker price is what's above water, but insurance, gas, maintenance, registration, repairs, and depreciation are underneath. People who only look at "can I afford the monthly payment?" crash into the iceberg.

Total Cost of Ownership (TCO) is the honest number. For most used cars, TCO over 5 years is roughly 1.5–2× the purchase price once you include fuel, insurance, maintenance, and depreciation.

Concept Buy new, buy used, or lease?

Buy Used (usually best)

  • Biggest depreciation hit already absorbed by previous owner
  • Cheaper insurance, cheaper registration (in many states)
  • You own an asset — sell whenever
  • Target 2–4 year old low-mileage from a private seller or reputable dealer

Lease

  • Lower monthly payment → looks cheap
  • Never build equity; permanent car payment
  • Mileage caps (10–15k/yr); overage = $0.15–$0.30/mile
  • Wear & tear charges at turn-in
  • Best only if you need a work car and write it off

Concept The 20 / 4 / 10 rule

A classic guardrail: put 20% down, finance for no more than 4 years, and keep total transportation costs (payment + insurance + gas + maintenance) under 10% of gross income. If you can't meet these, the car is probably too expensive for you right now.

Red Flag "Let's focus on the monthly payment"

This is the dealer's favorite phrase because it hides two things: the price of the car and the length of the loan. Stretch a $30,000 car over 84 months at 8% APR and the monthly payment looks "affordable" — and you pay about $38,000 total while driving a depreciated asset. Always negotiate the out-the-door price, the APR, and the term separately.

Concept The negotiation order (what to say, when)

  1. Get pre-approved for an auto loan from your bank or credit union before you walk in. Now you know the rate to beat.
  2. Negotiate the price only. Not the payment, not the trade-in yet, not financing. Just the price of this car.
  3. Then negotiate the trade-in as a separate transaction (or sell privately for ~20% more).
  4. Then compare dealer financing to your pre-approval. Take whichever is lower. They often beat it to make the sale.
  5. Decline add-ons in the F&I office: extended warranties, paint protection, wheel insurance, gap insurance (sometimes) — 50–90% markup. You can buy these elsewhere cheaper if you truly want them.

Smart Questions At the dealership

  1. What is the out-the-door price including all fees and taxes?
  2. Can I see the vehicle history report (Carfax / AutoCheck) for this specific VIN?
  3. Has it been inspected? Can I take it to my mechanic for a pre-purchase inspection ($100–$150)?
  4. What warranty comes with the car, and what does it actually cover?
  5. Is the APR rate I'm offered the best one I qualify for, or is it marked up?
  6. Are there any mandatory add-ons priced into this deal? (Walk away if any.)
  7. What's the return policy? Many used-car dealers offer a 3-day return.

Takeaway

Buy used. Pay cash when possible. If financing, get pre-approved first, cap at 48 months, and keep total car costs under 10% of gross income. The best car financially is the reliable one you already own.

Concept Renting isn't "throwing money away"

Renting is buying flexibility and predictability. Owning is buying stability and equity — with a side of surprise plumbing bills. Neither is morally superior. One of them is usually financially better for your specific situation.

The real question isn't "should I rent or buy forever?" It's "should I rent or buy for the next 5–7 years?" Because that's roughly how long you need to stay in a home for the transaction costs (6–10% to sell, 2–5% to buy) to break even.

Concept The hidden costs of owning

A mortgage payment is only the beginning. Expect to pay, every year:

  • Property tax — 0.5% to 2.5% of home value per year (varies by state)
  • Homeowners insurance — 0.3% to 1% of home value
  • Maintenance & repairs — budget 1% of home value per year (roofs, HVAC, water heaters all eventually die)
  • HOA fees — if applicable, $100–$700+/mo
  • PMI — if down payment is under 20%, add ~0.5–1% of loan/year until you reach 20% equity
  • Closing costs (one-time) — 2–5% of purchase price
  • Moving, furnishing, landscaping — easily $5,000–$20,000 in year 1

Concept Qualifying for a mortgage (the 5 Cs)

  • Credit — score of 620+ for conventional, 580+ FHA; 740+ gets the best rates
  • Capacity — DTI (debt-to-income) under 43%; lower is better
  • Capital — down payment + reserves (ideally 2–6 months of mortgage in savings after close)

Concept Boosting your odds of approval (and a better rate)

  1. Don't open new credit for 6–12 months before applying. Every hard inquiry dings you.
  2. Pay down credit cards to under 10% utilization 30–60 days before applying.
  3. Keep paystubs, W-2s, and tax returns handy — underwriters want 2 years of stable income (especially if self-employed).
  4. Don't job-hop or go freelance right before applying. Lenders want continuity.
  5. Season your down payment in your account for 60+ days. Surprise deposits (like a gift) need a documented paper trail.
  6. Shop 3+ lenders within a 14-day window — credit bureaus count that as one inquiry. Compare rates and fees (look at the Loan Estimate form).

Common Pitfalls First-time buyer mistakes

  1. Buying at the top of what you qualify for. The bank will approve you for more than is comfortable.
  2. Skipping the inspection to win a bidding war. A $400 inspection can uncover a $40,000 foundation problem. Never skip.
  3. Waiving the appraisal contingency without cash reserves to cover a shortfall.

  5. Emptying savings for the down payment. You need reserves for repairs, furnishings, and moving.

  2. What utilities and services are included?
  3. Is there a renters insurance requirement?
  4. What's the pet policy and are there pet fees/rent?
  5. How is the deposit returned and what can be deducted?
  6. Can I sublet or reassign if my situation changes?
  7. What's the rent renewal policy — max % increase typical?
  8. Who handles maintenance requests and what's the turnaround?

Smart Questions Before buying a home

  1. Can I honestly commit to staying 5+ years?
  2. Do I have down payment + closing + 2-month cushion + moving/repairs budget?
  3. What's my all-in PITI + HOA + maintenance, and is it < 28% of gross income?
  4. What's the school district, crime rate, commute, and neighborhood trajectory?
  5. Did the inspection find anything that changes the price — and did I ask?
  6. What's the seller's motivation — days on market, any concessions offered?
  7. Are there HOA restrictions I'd find intolerable?
  8. What's the flood / wildfire / disaster zone and insurance outlook?

Takeaway

Rent when your life is uncertain or the rent/buy math favors renting. Buy when you'll stay 5+ years, have real reserves, and the numbers work at conservative assumptions. Always inspect, always shop lenders, never max out your approval, and never skip the cushion.

Concept The order of operations

Think of investing like filling buckets that have different tax advantages. Fill them in the right order and you legally hand the IRS less money.

  1. Emergency fund first — 3–6 months of expenses in HYSA. Not investing; foundation.
  2. 401(k) up to the match — free salary. Skip this only if the match is zero.
  3. High-interest debt payoff — anything above ~8% APR beats market returns after risk.
  4. HSA (if eligible) — triple tax-advantaged; invest it, don't spend it from the account.
  5. Roth IRA to the annual limit (~$7,000 for most).
  6. Back to 401(k) to the annual limit (~$23,000).
  7. Taxable brokerage — flexible, no cap; capital gains taxed on sale.
  8. 529 plan if saving for someone's education.

Dollar limits change annually; check current on irs.gov.

Concept Roth vs. Traditional — the real question

Both accounts grow tax-free. The difference is when you pay tax.

Roth

  • Contribute after-tax dollars
  • Withdrawals in retirement are tax-free
  • Best if you expect higher tax bracket in retirement (or if you're young/low-income now)
  • Contributions (not earnings) can be pulled out anytime without penalty

Traditional

  • Contribute pre-tax dollars (deduction now)
  • Withdrawals in retirement are taxed as income
  • Best if you expect lower tax bracket in retirement (or if you're in a high bracket now)
  • Required Minimum Distributions start at 73

Rule of thumb for students and early-career: Roth wins. You're paying 12–22% tax now; you'll likely be at 24%+ later.

Concept Index funds vs. picking stocks

Over 20 years, roughly 90% of actively managed funds fail to beat a simple low-cost S&P 500 index fund after fees. The pros who do this full-time can't consistently beat the market — which is why the bet for amateurs is essentially always: own the whole market cheaply and don't touch it.

A 3-fund portfolio covers the world for most people:

  • US Total Market Index — e.g., VTI, SWTSX, FZROX
  • International Stock Index — e.g., VXUS, SWISX, FZILX
  • Bond Index — e.g., BND, SWAGX, FXNAX

Or even simpler: one target-date fund (e.g., "Target 2065") auto-adjusts the mix as you age. Not the cheapest, but hands-off.

Common Pitfalls Ways investors shoot themselves in the foot

  1. Panic-selling in a crash. You lock in the loss. Historically, the biggest gains come in the 12 months after a bottom.
  2. Market timing. Missing just the 10 best days in the market over 20 years cuts returns by more than half. Nobody knows which days those will be.
  3. Chasing last year's winner. Past performance is not a signal.
  4. Meme stocks & crypto as "investing." That's gambling — fine in small doses (≤ 5% of portfolio) as entertainment, not retirement.
  5. High expense ratios. 1% sounds small. Over 40 years, it steals roughly 25% of your final balance.
  6. Buying individual stocks on "tips." If it's in the news, it's already priced in.
  7. Raiding your 401(k) for a home or car. 10% penalty + taxes + lost compounding. Almost never worth it.

Concept Dollar-cost averaging (DCA)

DCA = investing the same amount every month regardless of market price. When prices are up, your $200 buys fewer shares. When prices crash, your $200 buys more. Over time, your average cost-per-share smooths out and you don't have to guess whether "now is a bad time."

It's like commuting to work. You don't only drive on sunny days — you drive every day, and the weather averages out.

Smart Questions Before buying any investment

  1. What's the expense ratio? (Aim under 0.10% for index funds.)
  2. Is this in a tax-advantaged account or a taxable one? (Put bonds in tax-advantaged if you can.)
  3. Does this duplicate something I already own?
  4. Would I still want to own this if the market dropped 40% tomorrow?
  5. What's my time horizon, and does this match it?
  6. Is the seller a fiduciary, or a commissioned salesperson?

Takeaway

The winning recipe is boring: capture the match, max the Roth, buy low-cost index funds, hold for decades. The more exciting your strategy, the more likely it underperforms. Set it up, stop checking, live your life.

Concept The two universes: federal vs. private

Federal loans are like renting from a landlord with a tenant-rights law. Private loans are renting with no lease — nobody has to be flexible if things go wrong.

Federal (Direct) Loans

  • Fixed interest rate set by Congress
  • Income-Driven Repayment (IDR) plans available
  • Potential forgiveness (PSLF, IDR, teacher, disability)
  • Deferment / forbearance during hardship
  • Discharged at death or total disability
  • No cosigner required

Private Loans

  • Rate varies by credit (often variable)
  • No IDR; monthly payment doesn't shrink if income drops
  • No federal forgiveness programs
  • Hardship options vary wildly by lender
  • Usually not discharged at death (cosigner on the hook)
  • Requires strong credit or cosigner

Rule: Exhaust federal Direct loans before any private loan. Every time.

Concept Income-Driven Repayment (IDR), simplified

IDR plans tie your monthly payment to your income — typically 10–20% of discretionary income (income above ~150% of federal poverty level). Balance not paid off by the end of the term (usually 20–25 years) is forgiven. Forgiven balance may be taxed as income.

The big IDR plans shift over time — SAVE, PAYE, IBR — and regulations change often. Check current rules at studentaid.gov before choosing.

Concept PSLF — the public-service shortcut

Public Service Loan Forgiveness forgives remaining federal Direct loan balance after 120 qualifying payments (≈10 years) while working for a qualifying public-service employer (government, 501(c)(3) nonprofit, some others). No taxes on the forgiven amount.

  • Only Direct loans qualify — consolidate FFEL into Direct if needed.
  • Submit Employment Certification Forms annually. Don't wait until year 10.
  • Combine with an IDR plan for the lowest payment (and highest final forgiven amount).
  • If you'll work in public service long-term: don't refinance to private — you'll lose eligibility.

Concept Refinancing (private) — when it helps, when it hurts

Refinance helps when…

  • Your loans are private (you have nothing to lose)
  • You have stable income and good credit
  • You can get a rate at least 1.5–2% lower than current
  • You won't need income-driven relief

Don't refinance federal loans when…

  • You might pursue PSLF or IDR forgiveness
  • Your income is variable or could drop
  • The rate improvement is under ~1%
  • You haven't built a 3–6 month emergency fund

Permanent tradeoff: once federal loans are refinanced to private, you cannot undo it. All federal protections are gone.

Common Mistakes

  1. Ignoring the loans during school — and watching unsubsidized interest capitalize.
  2. Minimum payments without a plan. The standard 10-year plan pays fastest; IDR minimizes short-term pain but can extend total paid.
  3. Refinancing federal → private for a small rate drop and losing PSLF eligibility.
  4. Missing annual IDR recertification — payment snaps back to the full Standard amount.
  5. Skipping scholarship searches your 2nd / 3rd / 4th years. Most scholarships are undersubscribed after freshman year.

Smart Questions Before taking out any student loan

  1. Have I exhausted federal Direct Subsidized → Unsubsidized → Work-study → scholarships → grants before considering private?
  2. What's the total I'll have borrowed by graduation — not just this semester?
  3. Is my expected starting salary at least equal to my total loan balance? (A common guardrail.)
  4. What's the interest rate — fixed or variable — and when does it start accruing?
  5. Is there a grace period before payments start?
  6. For private: is my cosigner released after a set number of on-time payments?

Takeaway

Federal loans are a safer currency than private loans — protect that by exhausting them first and never refinancing federal → private unless you're 100% sure you won't need IDR or PSLF. Make a plan before graduation; don't let the 6-month grace period end without knowing your first payment and strategy.

Concept What insurance actually is

Insurance is a group of people all chipping in a little to cover the person who has a disaster that month. You're not buying a "service"; you're buying protection against financial ruin from a low-probability, high-impact event.

Only insure what would financially wreck you, not what you can easily replace. Don't insure your phone; do insure your ability to work.

Concept Insurance vocabulary (the 5 words that matter)

  • Premium — what you pay (monthly/yearly) to have the policy.
  • Deductible — what you pay out of pocket before insurance kicks in. Higher deductible = lower premium.
  • Copay — flat dollar fee for a service (like $25 to see a doctor), paid alongside insurance.
  • Coinsurance — after deductible, your share of costs (e.g., "80/20" = insurance pays 80%, you pay 20%).
  • Out-of-pocket max — the yearly ceiling on what you'll pay. After this, insurance covers 100%.

The trap: people pick plans by premium only and forget the rest. A "cheap" plan with a $7,000 deductible can bankrupt you after one hospitalization.

Health Insurance Decoding plans (HMO, PPO, HDHP)

  • HMO — must use in-network providers; need PCP referrals. Cheapest premiums, most restrictive.
  • PPO — wider network, no referrals needed. More flexibility, higher premium.
  • HDHP — High Deductible Health Plan. Low premium, high deductible. Eligible to pair with an HSA. Good for healthy young people with a 3–6 month emergency fund.
  • EPO — in-network only like HMO, but no referrals needed.

HSA superpowers (only with HDHP): triple tax-advantaged (deductible in, grows tax-free, withdrawals for medical tax-free). Invest it once the balance is over ~$1,000; treat it as a stealth retirement account after 65 when it becomes like a traditional IRA.

Auto Insurance What each part actually does

  • Liability (required) — pays for damage you cause to others. Minimum state limits are usually too low; aim for 100/300/100 (bodily injury per person / per accident / property) or more.
  • Collision — fixes your car after a crash regardless of fault.
  • Comprehensive — non-collision damage (theft, weather, animal hits).
  • Uninsured / Underinsured Motorist — covers you if the other driver has no (or not enough) insurance. Critical — many drivers are underinsured.
  • Medical payments / PIP — medical costs for you and passengers.
  • Gap insurance — if you owe more than the car is worth and it's totaled, pays the "gap". Only worth it if you have a small down payment.

Drop collision & comprehensive once annual premium > ~10% of the car's market value. Instead, save that money.

Renters Insurance $15/mo that no one has a good reason to skip

Covers:

  • Your stuff — fire, theft, vandalism, certain water damage
  • Liability — if someone gets hurt in your place or you accidentally damage the building
  • Additional living expenses if your unit becomes unlivable

Typical policy: $20,000–$30,000 personal property + $100,000 liability for $120–$250/year. Landlords often require it now anyway. Almost everyone should have it.

Disability Insurance The one nobody talks about

Your ability to earn is usually your biggest asset. A 25-year-old earning $50K will make over $2M before retirement — but a disabling injury or illness can end that in a week.

  • Short-term disability — covers weeks to ~6 months. Often cheap or employer-provided.
  • Long-term disability — covers months to years or until retirement. This is the important one. Costs 1–3% of income; replaces ~60% of income if you can't work.
  • Own-occupation vs. any-occupation definitions matter. "Own-occupation" is stronger.

Life Insurance Term, not whole

Only need it if someone depends on your income (spouse, kids, dependent parents).

  • Term life — pure insurance, fixed premium for 10/20/30-year term. Very cheap when young and healthy.
  • Whole / universal life — insurance + savings + huge commissions for the salesperson. Almost never the right choice — the "investment" part returns poorly vs. just buying term and investing the difference.

Rule: coverage = 8–12× annual income, term = until kids are grown or mortgage is paid off.

Red Flags Insurance products to decline

  • Extended warranties — profit margins are huge. Save the money instead.
  • Credit life/disability — overpriced coverage on specific loans.
  • Rental car damage waivers — often already covered by your credit card or auto policy.
  • Whole life sold as an "investment" — it isn't.
  • Identity theft insurance — mostly sells you what you can do yourself (credit freezes, monitoring).

Smart Questions Before buying any policy

  1. What's the total annual cost (premium × 12)?
  2. What's the deductible and do I have it in savings?
  3. What's the out-of-pocket max for a catastrophic year?
  4. What's not covered? (Exclusions, waiting periods, network restrictions.)
  5. How does filing a claim actually work, and what affects my rates after?
  6. If I shop 3 providers for the same coverage, what's the range?
  7. Is this sold by a broker (shops multiple carriers) or an agent (one carrier only)?

Takeaway

Insurance is for catastrophes, not conveniences. Insure your health, your car's liability, your rental stuff, and your income. Skip the upsells. Shop every 2–3 years — loyalty is often punished with rate creep.

Concept Why side-income taxes sting

W-2 taxes are autopilot — your employer does the withholding behind the scenes. Side-income taxes are stick-shift — if you don't put the money aside yourself, you get flattened at tax time.

When you're paid as a contractor (1099-NEC) or self-employed, you owe:

  • Federal income tax on your profit (income − deductible expenses)
  • Self-employment tax (SE tax) — 15.3% (both halves of Social Security + Medicare, because there's no employer to pay the other half)
  • State income tax — if your state has one
  • Quarterly estimated payments — due ~April 15, June 15, Sept 15, Jan 15

The set-aside rule: park 25–30% of every side-income payment in a separate savings account labeled "Taxes." Touch it only to pay the IRS.

Concept Schedule C deductions (the legal ones)

Self-employment income goes on IRS Schedule C. You can subtract ordinary and necessary business expenses from your gross:

  • Mileage (~$0.67/mile for business driving — check current IRS rate)
  • Home office — a portion of rent/utilities if you have a dedicated space used exclusively for work
  • Software & subscriptions (Adobe, Zoom, your project-management tool)
  • Supplies & materials
  • Phone/internet — business-use portion
  • Professional development — courses, books, industry memberships
  • Payment processing fees (Stripe, PayPal, Square)
  • Self-employed health insurance — if you pay your own premiums (above-the-line deduction)
  • Half of SE tax — automatic above-the-line deduction

Keep receipts. A cheap expense-tracking app + a dedicated business checking account makes tax time 10× easier.

Concept Quarterly estimated payments

If you'll owe $1,000+ in taxes on side income, the IRS expects you to pay in quarters — not all at year-end. Missing them triggers underpayment penalties (small, but annoying).

Concept Retirement accounts for self-employed

  • Solo 401(k) — if you're self-employed with no employees. Contribute as both "employee" and "employer" — highest contribution limits.
  • SEP-IRA — simpler to set up, up to ~25% of net earnings. Good if you're a solo with variable income.
  • Roth IRA — still available to you (standard contribution limits apply).

Contributions reduce your self-employment taxable income, so they double as tax breaks.

Common Traps

  1. Spending the gross. If you treat every 1099 payment as "yours" without setting aside for tax, April catches you.
  2. Mixing personal + business money. Get a separate checking account from day one. Even a free one.
  3. Forgetting state tax. Federal isn't the only bill coming.
  4. Missing the hobby-vs-business line. If it's truly a business, you can deduct losses; if IRS calls it a hobby, you can't.
  5. Not tracking mileage. A free app logs every drive — often the single biggest deduction.
  6. LLC fever. For most solo side-hustlers, an LLC adds complexity without saving tax. Ask whether a liability concern actually exists.

Smart Questions When side income starts

  1. Do I have a separate business checking account?
  2. Am I tracking every expense (at minimum, a spreadsheet)?
  3. Am I setting aside 25–30% of each payment?
  4. Will I owe $1,000+ in taxes — meaning I should make quarterly payments?
  5. Should I form an LLC / S-corp? (Usually no until income exceeds ~$40K net.)
  6. Do I have an EIN? (Free from IRS; keeps SSN off invoices.)
  7. Am I documenting business vs. personal use clearly for mixed-use assets (car, phone, home)?

Takeaway

Side income is great — but the first person who gets paid is the IRS. Set aside 25–30% as soon as money lands. Track expenses. Pay quarterly if required. Tax software (or a cheap CPA at the first year) saves far more than it costs when you have 1099 income.

Concept The one principle

Negotiation isn't about winning or being aggressive. It's about being willing to pause — to say "let me think about it" instead of "yes" — while staying friendly. The person most willing to wait has the leverage.

Everything in this section is a script + a "walk-away signal." The walk-away is what makes the script work. If you can't actually walk, you're not negotiating, you're begging.

Concept Universal rules (apply to all of below)

  1. Never name the first number when you can help it. Ask for a range first.
  2. Anchor high (as buyer/employee) or low (as seller). The first number shapes the whole conversation.
  3. Silence is free. After they make an offer, wait 4–5 seconds. They often sweeten it to fill the gap.
  4. Get it in writing. "Great — can you send me that in an email so I have the details?"
  5. You have more information than you think. Levels.fyi (salary), Zillow + Redfin + private-sale comps (home), KBB + TrueCar (car), annualcreditreport.com (credit).
  6. The first "no" isn't a real no. It's often just the default script of whoever you're talking to. Ask who has authority to say yes.

💰 Playbook 1 Salary — new offer or raise

Prep: Look up the role/level/location on Levels.fyi, Glassdoor, Payscale, LinkedIn Salary. Identify the range.

New offer script:

"Thanks so much for the offer — I'm really excited about this role and the team. Based on my experience with [specific skills] and the market range I'm seeing for this role in [city], I was expecting something closer to [10–20% above their offer]. Is there flexibility to get to that number?"

Raise script:

"Over the last year I've [3 concrete wins with numbers where possible — shipped X, led Y, grew metric Z]. Based on that impact and the market for my role, I'd like to discuss moving my compensation to [target]. Can we talk about a path to get there?"

If they say "that's not possible": "I understand budget can be tight this cycle. What about [signing bonus / extra PTO / remote days / faster review cadence / title change]?"

Walk-away signal: "I want to make sure I make the right choice here, so I'd like to take a couple of days to think it over." Say it calmly, no ultimatum.

🚗 Playbook 2 Buying a car

Prep: KBB + Edmunds + TrueCar for fair market price. Pre-approval from your bank. Email 3–4 dealers the same request; respond to whoever comes back lowest.

Email opener to multiple dealers:

"Hi — I'm in the market for a [year/make/model/trim], ready to buy in the next 10 days. I'm contacting several dealerships for your best out-the-door price, all-in (taxes, fees, everything). Please reply with that single number and I'll compare."

On the lot (counter-script):

  • "Let's focus on the total out-the-door price, not monthly payments." → controls the frame
  • "I have pre-approval at X% APR. Can you beat that?" → then pick whichever wins
  • "I'd like to handle my trade-in separately" → keeps their price honest
  • "I'll pass on the extended warranty / paint protection / etch" → 4 times if needed

Walk-away signal: Stand up, smile, say "I really appreciate your time. Let me think about it." Hand them your card. 30% of the time they call you before the parking lot.

🏠 Playbook 3 Rent (signing or renewal)

Prep: Check current listings on the same building/block to find what new tenants are paying. Know vacancy rates in your area.

New-lease script (if price seems high):

"I'm very interested in the unit. I noticed similar 1-bedrooms in the building are listed at $X. If we can get to that number, I can sign today and put down the deposit."

Renewal script (landlord wants a big increase):

"I've been a reliable tenant for [X] years, never late. I'd like to stay, but a $Y increase would stretch my budget. Would you consider [smaller increase / flat renewal / 1 free month / signing a 2-year lease for a better rate]? Turnover costs you more than giving me a reasonable rate."

Walk-away signal: Start (visibly) touring other places. Mention one politely: "I'm looking at a unit on Oak St. — I'd rather stay, but I need to make the numbers work."

🏥 Playbook 4 Medical bills

Medical bills are the most negotiable bills in your life. Almost nobody pays sticker price if they push back.

Step 1 — Audit. Request an itemized bill. Errors are extremely common (duplicate charges, services never delivered, wrong codes). Flag anything suspicious.

Step 2 — Financial assistance. Every nonprofit hospital has a charity-care / financial-hardship program, often undiscussed. Apply regardless of income — many cover 100% for low/moderate income.

Step 3 — Negotiate the remainder.

"I want to pay this bill, but the current amount is a real hardship. I see your published self-pay rate is [often 30–50% lower]. Can you reduce this bill to that rate, or set up an interest-free payment plan?"

Step 4 — Offer cash. "If I could pay 50% today as full settlement, would you accept that?" Hospitals often do.

Don't: put medical bills on a credit card if you can avoid it. The hospital won't charge interest; the card will.

💳 Playbook 5 Credit card APR

Never negotiated your card's APR? Most people haven't. It works more often than you'd think.

Script:

"Hi, I've been a cardholder for [X] years and always pay on time. My current APR is [Y]%. I've received offers from other cards for significantly lower rates. Can you review my account for an APR reduction? I'd prefer to stay with you."

If the first rep can't help: "Is there a retention specialist I could speak with?" (yes, always.)

Same play works for annual fees: "I'm considering downgrading to a no-fee card. Is there a retention offer or fee waiver available?"

📱 Playbook 6 Recurring bills (internet, phone, streaming)

Once a year, call or chat-in:

"Hi, my bill has gone up to $X and I'm looking at switching to [competitor] at $Y. I'd rather stay, but I need my rate to be competitive. What promotional rates are available for existing customers?"

Magic words: "retention department," "cancel my service." Reps have discount ladders they can only offer when the system flags you as a cancellation risk.

Cancel & come back: some services offer the best deals to new customers. A new account every 12–24 months (if allowed) saves serious money.

Mistakes Common negotiation fails

  1. Not asking. The #1 reason people don't get better rates: they never tried.
  2. Getting emotional. Hostility kills leverage. Stay warm and curious.
  3. Naming your "max" first. They'll start just under it.
  4. Accepting "no" from someone without authority. Ask for a manager / retention / supervisor.
  5. Taking it personally when the answer is no. It's a business; it's not about you.
  6. Not preparing. Showing up with data is 80% of the battle.

Before any negotiation

  1. What's the market range? (Have 2–3 data points ready.)
  2. What's my target? My floor? My walk-away?
  3. What's my BATNA (Best Alternative To Negotiated Agreement)? The stronger your alternative, the stronger your position.
  4. What non-money items could sweeten the deal (PTO, equity, repair credits, fee waivers)?
  5. Am I talking to someone with authority to say yes?
  6. Can I afford to wait 48 hours for an answer?

Takeaway

Every hour you spend learning to negotiate is worth thousands. A single 30-minute call can shave a percent off a mortgage, add $5K to a salary, or cut a medical bill in half. Ask politely. Be willing to walk. Silence is free.

Time after a due date before interest or late fees apply.

Total earned before any deductions.

Credit check that affects your score (from loan/card applications).

Fund that owns a broad basket of stocks to mirror a market index (like the S&P 500).
Interest
The cost of borrowing money or the reward for lending it.

Individual Retirement Account. Roth = post-tax in, tax-free out. Traditional = pre-tax in, taxed out.

Something you owe. Debts are liabilities.

How quickly an asset can become cash without losing value. Checking = very liquid. Home = not.

The rate applied to your next dollar of income.

The smallest amount you can pay a lender without going late. Almost always a trap.
NCUA
The credit-union equivalent of FDIC insurance.

Take-home pay after all deductions.

Spending more than your account balance. Often triggers a $25–$35 fee.

The original amount borrowed, separate from interest.

Replacing an existing loan with a new one, often at a better rate.

Retirement account funded with after-tax dollars; withdrawals in retirement are tax-free.

Savings set aside for a known upcoming expense.
Stock
A share of ownership in a company.

An income range taxed at a specific marginal rate.

Dollar-for-dollar reduction of tax owed.

The length of time you have to repay a loan.

The process a lender uses to decide whether to approve your loan.

IRS form from an employer showing wages + withholding for the year.

Form you fill out at a new job to tell the employer how much tax to withhold.

Money your employer pulls from each paycheck and sends to the IRS on your behalf.

Return on an investment, usually expressed as a percentage.

An independent estimate of a home's market value, required by mortgage lenders.

Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement. Your backup plan; source of all negotiation leverage.

Fees paid at home purchase: title, appraisal, origination, taxes, etc. Usually 2–5% of price.

A contractual "out" in a home offer (inspection, appraisal, financing) that lets you withdraw without losing earnest money.

Flat dollar amount owed for a medical service regardless of deductible status.

What you pay out of pocket before insurance starts paying. Higher deductible = lower premium.

Debt-to-Income ratio. Monthly debt payments ÷ monthly gross income. Lenders usually want under 43%.
Escrow
Third party holds funds until all conditions of a contract are met. Also the account a lender uses for property taxes & insurance.

Government-backed mortgage with lower down payment (3.5%) and more flexible credit requirements.

High-Deductible Health Plan. Lower premiums, higher deductible. Required to contribute to an HSA.

Income-Driven Repayment. Federal student loan plans that cap payments at a percentage of discretionary income.
MIP
Mortgage Insurance Premium (FHA equivalent of PMI).
Out-of-pocket max
Yearly ceiling on your medical cost-sharing. After this, insurance pays 100%.
PITI
Principal, Interest, Taxes, Insurance — your real monthly mortgage payment (often plus HOA).
PMI
Private Mortgage Insurance. Required when down payment is under 20% on conventional loans; drops off at 20% equity.
Pre-approval
Lender review that confirms how much mortgage / auto loan you qualify for. Makes offers more credible.
Premium
The amount you pay for an insurance policy (monthly or annually).
PSLF
Public Service Loan Forgiveness. Federal program forgiving remaining Direct loan balance after 120 qualifying payments in public service.
Refinance
Replacing an existing loan with a new one, usually for a better rate or different term.
Schedule C
IRS form self-employed filers use to report business income and expenses.
SE tax
Self-Employment tax. 15.3% on self-employed earnings — you pay both halves of Social Security + Medicare.
Subsidized loan
Federal student loan where the government pays the interest while you're enrolled at least half-time.
Target-date fund
Single mutual fund that auto-shifts from stocks to bonds as you approach a target retirement year.
Term life
Life insurance that covers a fixed period (10/20/30 years) with level premiums. The cheap, sensible choice for most.
Title insurance
One-time insurance protecting against defects in a property's legal ownership history.
Underwriting
The lender's process of verifying your income, assets, and credit to approve a loan.
Uninsured motorist
Auto coverage that pays for your injuries/damages if another driver has no insurance.
Unsubsidized loan
Federal student loan where interest accrues from day 1 (including during school).
Whole life
Permanent life insurance combining coverage with a cash-value component. Usually a poor investment; rarely the right choice.

Next Steps Your 30-day plan

Knowledge without action is trivia. Pick the ones that apply to you, and do one a week.

  1. Week 1. Check your current accounts. List balances, interest rates, and fees. Note anything that looks wrong.
  2. Week 2. Open a no-fee HYSA (online bank) if you don't have one. Even $25 to start.
  3. Week 3. Set up ONE automatic transfer from checking to the HYSA on the day after payday — any amount.
  4. Week 4. Check your free credit report at annualcreditreport.com. Review for anything you don't recognize.

That's it. Four small moves. Every one of them compounds.
