Module 1
Meet Claude Cowork
From chatting to doing — understand what Cowork is, how it's set up, and how to run your first real task from start to finish.

What Cowork actually is

Standard Claude lives inside a chat window. You paste text in, Claude responds with text. That's powerful, but limited — you're always the bridge between Claude's advice and your actual files.

Cowork closes that gap. It gives Claude the ability to reach into your environment directly: open files, read their contents, make edits, create new documents, and produce real outputs — not drafts for you to copy-paste, but the actual file, done.

The core model

You describe a goal → Claude proposes a plan → you review and approve → Claude executes → you steer if needed. At every stage, you're in control. Claude never acts without your awareness.

How it differs from regular Claude

Regular Claude

Text in → text out. You copy the output and apply it yourself. Great for ideas, drafts, explanations.

Claude Cowork

Claude reads your actual files, executes tasks, and produces real outputs directly in your environment.

Setting up for the first time

  1. Install the Claude desktop app (macOS or Windows) from anthropic.com and sign in to your Anthropic account.
  2. Grant Cowork access to the folders it will work in. You control exactly which directories are in scope — nothing outside that boundary can be touched.
  3. Optionally connect your first plugin (e.g., browser or Microsoft 365) from the plugins panel.
  4. Write your first global instruction so Claude immediately understands your context (more on this in Module 2).
💡

Start narrow: Grant Cowork access to one project folder first, not your entire file system. Once you understand how it behaves, expand the scope. This also makes Claude's plan more focused and accurate from day one.

Running your first end-to-end task

The best first task is something concrete with a clear output — not "help me think about this," but "produce this specific thing." Here are good first tasks:

  • Summarize all the documents in this folder into one executive summary file
  • Read my project notes and draft a status update email
  • Review this code file and list every function that lacks documentation
  • Convert these 10 CSV files into a single formatted report
Example prompt
Read all the .md files in /projects/launch-notes/, summarize the key decisions made, and create a file called decisions-summary.md in the same folder.

How context influences Claude's planning

The quality of Cowork's output is directly tied to how well Claude understands your situation. When Claude can see your actual files, folder structure, naming conventions, and any global instructions you've set, its plans are dramatically more precise and its outputs require far less correction.

This is why Module 2 — customization — matters so much. The more standing context you give Claude up front, the less you need to explain in every session.

Module 2
Make It Yours
Global instructions, custom skills, and plugins transform Cowork from a general tool into a collaborator that knows you, your role, and how you work.

The three customization layers

Cowork's power isn't just what it can do by default — it's how deeply it can be shaped to your specific workflow. There are three complementary layers, each adding a different kind of knowledge:

🌐 Global Instructions

Who you are and how you prefer to work. Persistent across every session.

⚙️ Skills

Repeatable procedures you define. Triggered by you, executed consistently by Claude.

🔌 Plugins

Connections to specific apps and data. Extend what Claude can read, write, and do.

🔗 Together

All three stack. A skill can invoke a plugin, guided by your global instructions.

Global instructions — your permanent brief

Global instructions are a standing context document you write once and update over time. Every Cowork session reads this before it does anything. Think of it as the brief you'd give a new colleague on their first day — except this colleague never forgets it.

Good global instructions cover:

  • Your role and team context ("I'm a product manager at a 50-person SaaS company")
  • Your communication preferences ("Always use plain language, no jargon")
  • File conventions your team follows ("All meeting notes go in /notes/YYYY-MM-DD format")
  • Things to always or never do ("Never delete files — move them to /archive instead")
  • Preferred output formats ("Reports should always include an executive summary at the top")
Example global instruction
I'm a UX researcher at Acme Corp. My work lives in /research/. Files should use kebab-case naming. When writing reports, lead with a 2-sentence summary, then findings, then recommendations. Avoid academic language — write for busy stakeholders.
🔄

Treat global instructions as a living document. After each session where you had to correct Claude's style or format, add that correction to your global instructions so it doesn't happen again.

Skills — reusable workflows you define

A skill is a procedure that Claude follows every time you invoke it. Unlike one-off prompts, skills are saved, named, and reusable — and can be shared with your whole team.

Where skills shine:

  • Recurring reports: "Every Monday, read the weekly metrics file and generate a summary in the same format as last week's"
  • Consistent document transforms: "Take this raw interview transcript and extract quotes, themes, and action items into our standard template"
  • Code review checklists: "Review this file against our style guide and flag anything that doesn't follow our conventions"
  • Onboarding workflows: "Set up a new project folder with the standard subfolders and template files"

Skills are created by describing the workflow in natural language — no programming required. Claude stores the procedure and follows it each time you trigger that skill by name.

Plugins — extending Claude's reach

Plugins connect Cowork to specific apps and data sources beyond your local file system. Think of them as add-ons that expand the perimeter of what Claude can access and act on.

Common plugin categories include:

  • Browser plugins: Let Claude read and interact with web content in Chrome
  • Productivity suite plugins: Connect to Microsoft 365 (Word, Excel, Outlook, Teams)
  • Data source plugins: Connect to databases, spreadsheet services, or APIs
  • Communication plugins: Read and draft in email or messaging apps
Plugin safety note

Always validate a plugin before installing it. A plugin gets access to whatever it's designed to access — so review what permissions it requests and only install plugins from sources you trust. More on this in Module 4.

Module 3
Work Anywhere
Cowork doesn't stay in one window. With Chrome and Microsoft 365 integration, Claude follows you into the apps where you actually spend your day.

Why integration matters

Most knowledge work doesn't happen in a single app. You're in a browser researching, then in Word drafting, then in Outlook communicating, then in Excel analyzing. Each switch is friction — and traditionally, each switch also meant re-explaining your context to Claude.

Integrations eliminate that friction. Claude goes where you go, keeps its understanding of your work, and can act across app boundaries in a single workflow.

Chrome integration

The Claude browser integration lets Cowork read, summarize, and interact with content in your open browser tabs. This opens up a wide class of tasks that previously required copy-pasting everything into Claude first.

What you can do:

  • Research synthesis: "I have 8 tabs open from my research session — summarize the key findings across all of them into a single document"
  • Web form automation: Claude can fill out structured forms based on data you specify, useful for repetitive data entry
  • Competitive monitoring: "Read this competitor's pricing page and compare it to our current pricing in /sales/pricing.md"
  • Content review: "Read this blog post draft in my CMS tab and check it against our editorial style guide"
  • Data extraction: Pull structured information from web pages directly into local files or spreadsheets
🔒

The browser plugin can see content in open tabs — so be mindful of what you have open when running sensitive tasks. It only reads what it's explicitly asked to; it doesn't silently monitor your browsing.

Microsoft 365 integration

For organizations using Microsoft's productivity suite, the M365 integration brings Cowork directly into the apps where most document work happens. This isn't a sidecar — Claude can read and write within these applications.

Word & document workflows

  • Draft new sections of a document based on your notes and the document's existing style
  • Edit for clarity and tone without changing substance
  • Extract key information into a structured summary
  • Compare two document versions and write a changelog

Excel & data workflows

  • Clean and restructure messy data based on your specifications
  • Build or explain formulas in plain language
  • Summarize spreadsheet findings in narrative form
  • Cross-reference data across multiple sheets or files

Outlook & Teams workflows

  • Summarize long email threads and surface the decision that needs to be made
  • Draft replies that match your tone and include relevant context from your files
  • Turn meeting notes from Teams into action items assigned to the right people
  • Write follow-up emails from meeting transcripts
Cross-app workflow example
Read the email thread in Outlook labeled "Q3 Budget Review", then open the Q3-budget.xlsx file, update the summary tab to reflect the decisions made in the thread, and draft a reply to the thread confirming the changes.
The cross-app advantage

The real leverage comes from chaining apps together in a single instruction — something that previously would have required switching tools manually a dozen times. Claude can hold context across the whole workflow.

Module 4
Sharing & Safety
With the ability to act on files and apps comes the need for good judgment about trust, verification, and team governance.

Why safety thinking matters here

Claude Cowork is significantly more powerful than a chat interface — and that means mistakes or misplaced trust have more tangible consequences. A wrong answer in a chat is just words. An incorrect file edit, a plugin with bad permissions, or an action you didn't review properly can affect real work.

None of this is a reason to avoid Cowork — it's a reason to build good habits from the start.

Review before execution

Cowork is designed to show you what it plans to do before it does it. This is your most important safety lever. Always:

  • Read Claude's proposed plan before approving it, especially for tasks that write or delete files
  • Spot-check the scope — make sure Claude hasn't planned to touch files or folders outside what you intended
  • Ask Claude to clarify any step that isn't clear ("what exactly will you change in that file?")
  • Use a test folder for experiments before running a workflow on important files
🛡️

The archive rule: A simple global instruction — "never delete files, move them to /archive instead" — gives you a recovery path for anything Cowork touches. Always run new workflows with this in place until you're confident.

Plugin validation

Not all plugins are created equal, and some may request more access than they need. Before installing any plugin:

  1. Check who published it and whether it comes from a source your organization trusts
  2. Review exactly what permissions it requests — does it need access to things it has no reason to touch?
  3. Look for documentation explaining what data the plugin sends externally, if any
  4. Test it with non-sensitive content first before using it in important workflows
  5. Remove plugins you no longer use — unused access is unnecessary risk
For teams

Establish an approved plugin list for your organization. Individuals installing unreviewed plugins with broad permissions creates risks that extend beyond the individual user — especially when those plugins have access to shared file systems or email.

Sensitive data considerations

Cowork sends content to Anthropic's API to process it — the same as regular Claude chat. This means:

  • Content in files Claude reads is sent to Anthropic for processing (review Anthropic's privacy policy and your organization's AI usage policy)
  • Don't use Cowork to process data you're contractually or legally prohibited from sending to third-party AI services
  • Be especially careful with PII, health data, financial records, or anything covered by compliance requirements (HIPAA, GDPR, etc.)
  • Scope folder access narrowly — only grant Claude access to what it needs for the current work

Team sharing — skills and plugins

One of Cowork's most valuable features for teams is shared configuration: everyone using the same validated plugins and battle-tested skills means consistency and lower onboarding cost.

Best practices for team sharing:

  • Shared global instructions: Define a team-level global instruction that establishes your shared conventions, then let individuals extend it with personal preferences
  • Shared skill library: Maintain a set of approved, tested skills for common recurring tasks so people don't reinvent them
  • Plugin governance: Designate someone responsible for reviewing and approving plugins before they're shared team-wide
  • Onboarding: New team members can get productive faster if they inherit tested skills and a good global instruction from day one
📋

Treat your team's shared Cowork configuration the same way you'd treat shared infrastructure: version-controlled, documented, reviewed before changes, and with a clear owner. It's the foundation everyone's workflows are built on.

Real-world application

Cowork in a Campaign

Whether you're running a product launch, marketing push, fundraising drive, or institutional initiative — Cowork can reshape how the work gets done at every stage.

Campaigns are high-stakes, high-volume, time-compressed. They involve dozens of moving parts: briefs, content, data, stakeholder updates, performance reviews, narrative pivots. Most campaign teams spend enormous energy on coordination and formatting tasks that could be automated — leaving less time for the strategic thinking that actually determines outcomes.

Claude Cowork changes that calculus. It doesn't replace campaign judgment — it removes the friction around it, so teams can focus on the decisions that matter: what story to tell, who to tell it to, and when to change course.

The automation opportunity in campaigns

A significant portion of campaign work is procedural: generating the same report in a new week's data, reformatting the same content for a different channel, compiling the same stakeholder briefing from scattered inputs. These tasks are necessary but not strategic — and they consume hours that should go toward judgment calls.

Cowork can handle the procedural layer. Define a workflow once (as a Skill), point it at your current files, and it runs. What used to take a morning can take minutes.

What campaigns can automate with Cowork

  • Weekly performance digests: Read the latest metrics export, compare to last week's baseline, and draft a summary email with highlights and flags — every Monday morning, same format, zero manual work.
  • Content repurposing pipeline: Take a single long-form piece (blog post, white paper, speech) and produce channel-adapted versions for LinkedIn, email newsletter, social media, and press release — all in one Cowork instruction.
  • Stakeholder update generation: Read the week's project files, action item logs, and milestone tracker, then draft a crisp update in whatever format your stakeholders expect — no more starting from a blank page.
  • Email sequence production: From a campaign brief and product information file, generate a multi-email nurture sequence with consistent voice, escalating specificity, and appropriate CTAs for each stage.
  • Meeting notes → action items → follow-ups: Feed in a raw transcript or notes file and get a structured action item list, owner assignments (based on names in the doc), and a follow-up email draft — all at once.
  • Asset inventory and audit: Scan a campaign folder, list every asset, flag anything missing from the required checklist, and output a status table ready to share.

Turn it into a Skill

Once you've run an automation task once and like the output, save it as a Cowork Skill. From then on, a single trigger runs the whole workflow — no re-explaining required. This is where automation compounds: the second time takes seconds, not minutes.

The analysis bottleneck

Campaigns generate data constantly — impressions, clicks, conversions, costs, responses, sentiment. The bottleneck isn't the data; it's the interpretation. By the time someone has pulled the numbers, formatted a table, and written a narrative around it, the moment to act may have passed.

Cowork compresses the gap between raw data and actionable insight. Point it at your exports and ask for analysis in plain language — no dashboard configuration, no formula writing, no waiting for a weekly report.

What campaigns can analyze with Cowork

  • Cross-channel performance synthesis: Feed in exports from three different platforms and ask Claude to identify the common threads — what's working regardless of channel, and what's underperforming everywhere.
  • Audience segmentation: Read a contact list or CRM export and group contacts by engagement pattern, geography, or behavior — then recommend a different follow-up strategy for each segment.
  • A/B test interpretation: Compare two campaign variants (subject lines, landing pages, ad copy) and get a plain-language explanation of which won, by how much, and what the result suggests for the next test.
  • Competitive positioning analysis: Read saved competitor pages, press releases, or ad copies and produce a comparative matrix: what they're emphasizing, what they're avoiding, where your campaign has a gap or an edge.
  • Trend detection: Look across multiple weeks of campaign data and flag anything that's moving significantly — positive or negative — before it becomes obvious in a monthly review.
  • Budget efficiency analysis: Read spend data alongside performance data and calculate cost-per-outcome by channel, campaign, or audience segment — identifying where budget should shift.

The narrative challenge in campaigns

Every campaign has a core story — why this, why now, why us, why it matters. Keeping that story sharp and consistent across a dozen pieces of content, three channels, five team members, and an eight-week run is genuinely hard. Drift happens: the email sounds different from the landing page, the social posts lose the thread, the deck for leadership uses different framing than the deck for customers.

Cowork can act as a narrative consistency layer — checking content against your source-of-truth messaging, adapting your core story for different audiences, and drafting new content that stays on-brief without requiring a senior writer on every output.

What campaigns can articulate better with Cowork

  • Messaging consistency audits: Run every piece of campaign content against your brand messaging guide and get a flagged report: what's on-brand, what's drifted, and specific suggested edits to bring it back.
  • Audience-adapted narrative: Start from one master campaign brief and produce targeted versions of the core story for different audiences — technical vs. executive, existing customers vs. prospects, B2B vs. consumer — each preserving the central message while speaking the right language.
  • Campaign brief writing: Feed in strategy documents, research notes, and a rough objective and have Cowork produce a polished campaign brief in your standard template — ready for the team to align on before any content is written.
  • Performance-to-story translation: Take dry metrics and campaign results and have Cowork write the narrative version — the story of what happened, what it means, and what it sets up — for leadership presentations, board updates, or post-campaign reports.
  • Pitch and proposal drafting: Read your campaign results, the client brief, and relevant case studies, then draft a proposal or pitch narrative that connects all three into a coherent argument.
  • Thought leadership pipeline: Pull insights from internal documents — strategy memos, research notes, retrospectives — and turn them into polished thought leadership drafts that reflect the genuine expertise inside your team.
